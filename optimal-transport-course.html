A beginner course

Optimal transport, from Monge to Sinkhorn

A self-contained tour of optimal transport for someone comfortable with measure theory and convex optimization. We build from Monge's 1781 problem through the Wasserstein distance, Kantorovich duality, and the Sinkhorn algorithm, then turn the machinery onto Wasserstein GANs, domain adaptation, and aligning neural recordings across subjects.

Audience
Researchers and grad students with linear algebra, measure-theoretic probability, and convex optimization in their toolkit.
Time
About 6 hours read-through, longer with the exercises and code.
Prerequisites
Comfortable with $\sigma$-algebras, push-forwards $T_\#\mu$, weak convergence of measures, and Lagrangian duality. Python for the hands-on section.
Goal
By the end, you should be able to read modern OT papers, run computational experiments with POT, and judge when OT is the right tool for a problem.

Module 0 · Why optimal transport?

Suppose you have two probability distributions $\mu$ and $\nu$ on $\mathbb{R}^d$ and you want to say how different they are. The textbook answer is some $f$-divergence — KL, total variation, $\chi^2$, Hellinger. They share a problem: they are blind to geometry.

Take two Diracs, $\mu = \delta_0$ and $\nu_\theta = \delta_\theta$. For any $\theta \neq 0$, $\mathrm{KL}(\mu \| \nu_\theta) = +\infty$ and $\mathrm{TV}(\mu, \nu_\theta) = 1$. Whether $\theta = 10^{-6}$ or $\theta = 10^{6}$ doesn't change the answer. That is fine for testing equality, but useless if you want a smooth signal to optimize against — exactly the situation in generative modelling, where you compare an empirical sample distribution to a model.

Optimal transport gives you a metric that knows where mass is. The Wasserstein distance between $\delta_0$ and $\delta_\theta$ is $|\theta|$. It is shaped by the cost of moving mass through the underlying space, which is exactly what you want when the geometry of that space is meaningful.

The transport metaphor

Imagine $\mu$ as piles of sand at various locations and $\nu$ as the holes you need to fill. Each unit of sand moved from $x$ to $y$ costs $c(x, y)$ (typically $\|x - y\|^p$). The optimal-transport problem asks for the cheapest plan that empties every pile and fills every hole. The cost of that plan is your distance.

What you can do with OT

Throughout the course we'll see the three application threads you flagged:

  • Computational. The Sinkhorn algorithm and its variants make OT scale to millions of samples. The POT Python library is the standard toolkit.
  • Machine learning. Wasserstein GANs use the geometric metric to stabilize generative training. Optimal-transport domain adaptation (OTDA) reweights source samples to match a target distribution.
  • Neuroscience. Gromov–Wasserstein OT aligns neural recordings across subjects, sessions, or modalities without paired data — only the internal geometry of each recording is used.

Roadmap

Modules 1–3 lay the mathematical foundations: the primal problem, its relaxation, the metric, and the dual. Module 4 is the computational core — entropic regularization and Sinkhorn — which is where modern OT lives in practice. Module 5 covers the continuous case (Brenier's theorem). Modules 6–7 are applications and code. Module 8 points you at the literature.

Module 1 · Monge & Kantorovich

1.1 Monge's problem (1781)

Gaspard Monge, an engineer working for Napoleon's army, asked: given a pile of dirt with mass distribution $\mu$ and a target trench shape $\nu$, what is the cheapest way to push the dirt? Formally, given probability measures $\mu$ on $X$ and $\nu$ on $Y$ and a cost $c \colon X \times Y \to \mathbb{R}_+$, find a transport map $T \colon X \to Y$ that pushes $\mu$ to $\nu$ — that is, $T_\#\mu = \nu$, meaning $\mu(T^{-1}(B)) = \nu(B)$ for every measurable $B$ — minimizing

$$\inf_T \int_X c\bigl(x, T(x)\bigr)\, d\mu(x) \quad \text{subject to } T_\#\mu = \nu. \tag{Monge}$$

Two things go wrong with this formulation:

  1. Existence can fail. If $\mu = \delta_0$ and $\nu = \tfrac{1}{2}\delta_{-1} + \tfrac{1}{2}\delta_{+1}$, no map $T$ can split a single point into two. Monge's problem is empty here.
  2. Even when maps exist, the constraint $T_\#\mu = \nu$ is highly non-convex. There is no clean Lagrangian, no clean optimization theory.

1.2 Kantorovich's relaxation (1942)

Leonid Kantorovich's insight was to allow mass splitting. Instead of a deterministic map, consider a joint distribution $\pi$ on $X \times Y$ whose marginals are $\mu$ and $\nu$. We call such a $\pi$ a coupling or transport plan, and the set of couplings is

$$\Pi(\mu, \nu) = \{\pi \in \mathcal{P}(X \times Y) : (\mathrm{proj}_X)_\#\pi = \mu,\ (\mathrm{proj}_Y)_\#\pi = \nu\}.$$

The Kantorovich problem is

$$\boxed{\,\mathrm{OT}_c(\mu, \nu) = \inf_{\pi \in \Pi(\mu, \nu)} \int_{X \times Y} c(x, y)\, d\pi(x, y).\,} \tag{K}$$

Why this works: $\Pi(\mu, \nu)$ is always non-empty (the product measure $\mu \otimes \nu$ is in it), the constraints are linear in $\pi$, and the objective is linear in $\pi$. It's a linear program. When $X, Y$ are compact and $c$ is continuous, Prokhorov's theorem gives compactness of $\Pi(\mu, \nu)$ in the weak-$*$ topology, so the infimum is attained.

Monge inside Kantorovich

Every Monge map $T$ corresponds to a deterministic Kantorovich plan $\pi_T = (\mathrm{id}, T)_\#\mu$, supported on the graph of $T$. Kantorovich is a relaxation: $\inf(\text{K}) \leq \inf(\text{Monge})$. A central theme of the theory — Brenier's theorem in Module 5 — is identifying conditions under which the optimal $\pi$ is in fact concentrated on the graph of a map, recovering the Monge solution.

1.3 The discrete case is just an LP

When $\mu = \sum_{i=1}^n a_i \delta_{x_i}$ and $\nu = \sum_{j=1}^m b_j \delta_{y_j}$ with $\sum a_i = \sum b_j = 1$, a coupling is an $n \times m$ matrix $P$ with non-negative entries and prescribed marginals. Writing $C_{ij} = c(x_i, y_j)$, the problem becomes

$$\min_{P \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times m}_+} \langle C, P \rangle \quad \text{s.t.}\quad P\mathbf{1}_m = a,\ P^\top \mathbf{1}_n = b. \tag{LP}$$

This is a transportation linear program with $nm$ variables and $n + m$ equality constraints (one of which is redundant since $\sum a_i = \sum b_j$). It can be solved exactly with the network simplex method in roughly $O(n^3 \log n)$ for $n = m$. The Hungarian algorithm handles the special case $a = b = \tfrac{1}{n}\mathbf{1}$ with permutation solutions.

This is the form OT takes in essentially every practical computation: empirical distributions, $a_i = 1/n$, cost matrix $C_{ij} = \|x_i - y_j\|^p$.

Module 2 · The Wasserstein distance

For $p \geq 1$, the $p$-Wasserstein distance between $\mu, \nu \in \mathcal{P}_p(X)$ (probability measures with finite $p$-th moment on a metric space $(X, d)$) is

$$W_p(\mu, \nu) = \left( \inf_{\pi \in \Pi(\mu, \nu)} \int d(x, y)^p \, d\pi(x, y) \right)^{1/p}.$$

This is exactly the Kantorovich problem with cost $c = d^p$, raised to the $1/p$. It deserves its own name because of three properties.

2.1 It's a metric

$W_p$ is a metric on $\mathcal{P}_p(X)$:

  • $W_p(\mu, \nu) \geq 0$, with equality iff $\mu = \nu$ (uses positivity of the cost and that the only coupling with zero $d^p$-integral is the diagonal).
  • Symmetry: swap the roles of $X$ and $Y$ in any coupling.
  • Triangle inequality: this is the work. The standard "gluing lemma" composes a coupling of $\mu, \rho$ with one of $\rho, \nu$ to produce a coupling of $\mu, \nu$. Minkowski on $L^p$ then gives the inequality.

2.2 It metrizes weak convergence (plus moments)

For $\mu_n, \mu \in \mathcal{P}_p(\mathbb{R}^d)$, $W_p(\mu_n, \mu) \to 0$ if and only if $\mu_n \rightharpoonup \mu$ and $\int \|x\|^p\, d\mu_n \to \int \|x\|^p\, d\mu$. So Wasserstein controls both shape and tails — a property KL doesn't give you.

2.3 It has a geodesic structure (Wasserstein space)

$(\mathcal{P}_p(\mathbb{R}^d), W_p)$ is a geodesic space. For $W_2$ on Euclidean space, McCann's displacement interpolation gives the geodesic between $\mu_0$ and $\mu_1$:

$$\mu_t = \bigl((1-t)\,\mathrm{id} + t\, T\bigr)_\#\mu_0, \quad t \in [0, 1],$$

where $T$ is the optimal Monge map (Brenier, Module 5). This is mass moving along straight lines, not mass cross-fading like the linear interpolation $(1-t)\mu_0 + t\mu_1$ does. Displacement interpolation is the basis for diffusion and flow models that interpolate between distributions.

Module 3 · Duality

The Kantorovich problem is a linear program in the space of measures. Its LP dual gives a powerful alternate formulation, especially for $W_1$.

3.1 Kantorovich duality

The dual of (K) is

$$\mathrm{OT}_c(\mu, \nu) = \sup_{(\varphi, \psi)} \left\{ \int \varphi\, d\mu + \int \psi\, d\nu : \varphi(x) + \psi(y) \leq c(x, y) \right\}, \tag{D}$$

where $\varphi \in L^1(\mu)$, $\psi \in L^1(\nu)$. The functions $(\varphi, \psi)$ are called Kantorovich potentials.

Strong duality holds whenever $c$ is lower semicontinuous and bounded below — this is a result of Kellerer's. Optimal potentials are $c$-conjugate: $\psi(y) = \inf_x \{c(x,y) - \varphi(x)\}$ and vice versa.

3.2 The Kantorovich–Rubinstein theorem (W$_1$)

When $c(x, y) = d(x, y)$ is a metric, the dual collapses dramatically. The constraint $\varphi(x) + \psi(y) \leq d(x, y)$ together with $c$-conjugacy forces $\psi = -\varphi$ and $\varphi$ to be 1-Lipschitz with respect to $d$. The dual becomes

$$\boxed{\,W_1(\mu, \nu) = \sup_{\|\varphi\|_{\mathrm{Lip}} \leq 1} \int \varphi\, d\mu - \int \varphi\, d\nu.\,}$$

This is the Kantorovich–Rubinstein formula. It says the $W_1$ distance is the largest expectation-difference you can produce with a 1-Lipschitz "test function" $\varphi$. We will see in the WGAN section that this is a parametrizable optimization — fit a neural network with a Lipschitz constraint and you have a $W_1$ estimator.

3.3 Optimality conditions and complementary slackness

If $\pi^\star$ and $(\varphi^\star, \psi^\star)$ are optimal, then

$$\varphi^\star(x) + \psi^\star(y) = c(x, y) \quad \pi^\star\text{-a.s.}$$

The support of $\pi^\star$ is contained in the $c$-cyclical monotone set $\{(x, y) : \varphi^\star(x) + \psi^\star(y) = c(x, y)\}$. In the discrete case, this is the standard LP complementary slackness on $C_{ij} - \varphi_i - \psi_j \geq 0$ with equality on the support.

Why duality matters in practice

The primal has $nm$ variables for $n$ source points and $m$ target points — quadratic. The dual has only $n + m$ variables. Dual ascent methods (which Sinkhorn essentially is, in disguise) operate in the dual.

Module 4 · Computation & Sinkhorn

Solving (LP) exactly is feasible up to $n \approx 10^4$ on a laptop but quickly becomes prohibitive. The trick that unlocked OT for modern data sizes is entropic regularization, due to Cuturi (2013).

4.1 Entropic regularization

Add a relative-entropy penalty to the objective:

$$\mathrm{OT}_c^\varepsilon(\mu, \nu) = \min_{\pi \in \Pi(\mu, \nu)} \int c\, d\pi + \varepsilon\, H(\pi \| \mu \otimes \nu),$$

where $H(\pi \| \rho) = \int \log \tfrac{d\pi}{d\rho}\, d\pi$. In the discrete case with reference $\mu \otimes \nu$ replaced by Lebesgue, this is

$$\min_{P \in \Pi(a, b)} \langle C, P \rangle + \varepsilon \sum_{ij} P_{ij}(\log P_{ij} - 1).$$

Two reasons this is a great idea:

  • Strong convexity. The objective becomes strongly convex in $P$, so the minimizer is unique.
  • Closed-form structure. The Lagrangian over the marginal constraints gives

$$P^\star_{ij} = u_i\, K_{ij}\, v_j, \quad K_{ij} = \exp(-C_{ij}/\varepsilon),$$

for some non-negative scaling vectors $u, v$. The whole problem reduces to finding $u, v$ such that $\mathrm{diag}(u) K \mathrm{diag}(v)$ has marginals $a, b$. This is a matrix scaling problem.

4.2 The Sinkhorn algorithm

The matrix-scaling problem is solved by alternating projection. Start with any positive $v^{(0)}$. Iterate

$$u^{(t+1)} = \frac{a}{K v^{(t)}}, \qquad v^{(t+1)} = \frac{b}{K^\top u^{(t+1)}},$$

where division is element-wise. Each step makes one marginal exact at the cost of disturbing the other. The iteration converges linearly (in fact, with rate equal to the contraction factor of the Hilbert projective metric on the positive cone). After convergence, $P^\star = \mathrm{diag}(u) K \mathrm{diag}(v)$.

In log-domain, the same iteration becomes a softmin over potentials:

$$\varphi_i^{(t+1)} = \varepsilon \log a_i - \varepsilon \mathrm{logsumexp}_j\bigl((\psi_j^{(t)} - C_{ij})/\varepsilon\bigr),$$

and analogously for $\psi$. Log-domain is essential for small $\varepsilon$ where $K_{ij} = e^{-C_{ij}/\varepsilon}$ underflows.

4.3 The Sinkhorn divergence

The entropic plan is biased: $\mathrm{OT}_c^\varepsilon(\mu, \mu) \neq 0$ in general. The fix is the Sinkhorn divergence:

$$S_\varepsilon(\mu, \nu) = \mathrm{OT}^\varepsilon(\mu, \nu) - \tfrac{1}{2}\mathrm{OT}^\varepsilon(\mu, \mu) - \tfrac{1}{2}\mathrm{OT}^\varepsilon(\nu, \nu).$$

This interpolates between $W_p^p$ at $\varepsilon = 0$ and the maximum-mean discrepancy MMD$_K$ at $\varepsilon = \infty$. It is positive, $S_\varepsilon(\mu, \nu) = 0 \Leftrightarrow \mu = \nu$, and differentiable — a workhorse loss for generative modelling.

4.4 Practical considerations

  • Choose $\varepsilon$ on the cost scale. Rule of thumb: $\varepsilon \approx 0.05 \cdot \mathrm{median}(C)$.
  • Use log-domain for any non-trivial problem.
  • Stop early. Sinkhorn doesn't need to fully converge for downstream gradient signals.
  • GPU it. Sinkhorn is just matrix-vector products. Libraries like geomloss (Feydy) and POT's GPU backend run it on millions of samples.

Module 5 · Brenier & continuous OT

For the squared-Euclidean cost $c(x, y) = \tfrac{1}{2}\|x - y\|^2$ on $\mathbb{R}^d$, the structure of the optimal coupling is remarkably clean.

5.1 Brenier's theorem

Suppose $\mu, \nu \in \mathcal{P}_2(\mathbb{R}^d)$ and $\mu$ is absolutely continuous with respect to Lebesgue measure. Then there exists a unique optimal Monge map $T = \nabla \phi$, where $\phi \colon \mathbb{R}^d \to \mathbb{R}$ is convex. Equivalently, the optimal Kantorovich plan is supported on the graph of $\nabla \phi$.

Convexity of $\phi$ is forced by $c$-cyclical monotonicity: an optimal coupling supports pairs $(x, y)$ that are monotone in the sense that no swap reduces total cost. For squared cost on $\mathbb{R}^d$, this monotonicity coincides with $T = \nabla \phi$ being the gradient of a convex function.

5.2 The Monge–Ampère equation

If $\mu = f(x)\,dx$ and $\nu = g(y)\,dy$ are smooth densities, the change-of-variables for $T = \nabla \phi$ gives

$$\det(D^2 \phi(x)) = \frac{f(x)}{g(\nabla \phi(x))}.$$

This is a fully nonlinear PDE (Monge–Ampère). It's important theoretically and increasingly computationally — neural OT solvers parametrize $\phi$ as an input-convex neural network and learn it directly.

5.3 Continuous Sinkhorn and Schrödinger bridges

The entropic regularization of continuous OT is intimately related to Schrödinger's problem: find the most likely random path of a Brownian particle conditioned to start with distribution $\mu$ and end with $\nu$. The optimal entropic plan converges to the Brenier solution as $\varepsilon \to 0$, with corrections of order $\varepsilon \log \varepsilon$. This connection has driven a wave of work on diffusion-based generative models and flow matching.

Module 6 · Applications

6.1 Wasserstein GANs

Arjovsky, Chintala & Bottou (2017) replaced the JS-divergence in the original GAN objective with the Wasserstein-1 distance. By Kantorovich–Rubinstein,

$$W_1(\mathbb{P}_r, \mathbb{P}_g) = \sup_{\|f\|_{\mathrm{Lip}} \leq 1} \mathbb{E}_{x \sim \mathbb{P}_r}[f(x)] - \mathbb{E}_{x \sim \mathbb{P}_g}[f(x)].$$

Parametrize $f$ as a neural network (the critic) and the generator $g_\theta$ that produces $\mathbb{P}_g$, and the WGAN minimax becomes

$$\min_\theta \max_{\|f_w\|_{\mathrm{Lip}} \leq 1} \mathbb{E}_{x \sim \mathbb{P}_r}[f_w(x)] - \mathbb{E}_{z \sim \mathbb{P}_z}[f_w(g_\theta(z))].$$

The Lipschitz constraint is the practical sticking point. The original paper used weight clipping (clamp critic weights to $[-c, c]$). Gulrajani et al. (2017) introduced the gradient penalty, adding $\lambda \mathbb{E}[(\|\nabla_{\hat x} f_w(\hat x)\|_2 - 1)^2]$ to the critic loss, evaluated on samples $\hat x$ along straight lines between real and fake. Spectral normalization (Miyato 2018) is the modern alternative.

Empirically, WGAN-GP gives a smooth meaningful loss (the critic loss tracks $W_1$, so it correlates with sample quality) and avoids the mode-collapse pathologies of vanilla GANs. The price is needing more critic updates per generator update — the critic must be near-optimal for the Wasserstein estimate to be useful.

6.2 Domain adaptation with OT

Setup: labelled source $\{(x_i^s, y_i^s)\}$ and unlabelled target $\{x_j^t\}$ from different distributions. Goal: classify the target.

Courty et al. (2017) proposed: compute the OT plan $P^\star$ between source and target empirical distributions. Then transform each source point to its barycentric image in the target,

$$\hat x_i^s = \arg\min_x \sum_j P^\star_{ij}\, c(x, x_j^t) = \frac{\sum_j P^\star_{ij}\, x_j^t}{\sum_j P^\star_{ij}}\quad (\text{quadratic cost}),$$

and train a classifier on the transported labelled set $\{(\hat x_i^s, y_i^s)\}$. The OT plan can be regularized with a class-aware term that encourages source points of the same class to map to nearby target points.

JDOT (Courty 2017) goes further: jointly transport features and labels, optimizing classifier and coupling together. This leads naturally to deep OT domain adaptation methods, which add a Wasserstein-distance term between source and target feature distributions to a classifier's loss.

6.3 Aligning neural recordings

Neural decoding research often runs into a basic problem: recordings from different subjects, sessions, or even probe insertions have different latent geometries. Naively pooling data fails. The classical fix is hyperalignment (Haxby 2011) — but it requires shared stimuli, paired data.

Gromov–Wasserstein for unsupervised alignment. When you have two point clouds in possibly different ambient spaces, with cost matrices $C^X$ on space $X$ and $C^Y$ on space $Y$, the Gromov–Wasserstein distance is

$$\mathrm{GW}^2(\mu, \nu) = \inf_{\pi \in \Pi(\mu, \nu)} \int \bigl(C^X(x, x') - C^Y(y, y')\bigr)^2\, d\pi(x, y)\, d\pi(x', y').$$

It minimizes distortion of pairwise distances rather than transport cost — so it doesn't need a common space. Two recordings of $n$ neurons each, summarized as $n \times n$ correlation matrices, can be aligned with GW even when neurons are unidentified across subjects. This is exactly the setup of recent work using GW-OT for fMRI alignment (Thual et al., Bazeille et al.) and for the open-source GW-OT toolbox introduced in Sasaki et al. (2025, J. Neurosci. Methods).

OT for trial-to-trial neural variability. Each trial gives a population firing-rate vector or low-dimensional latent state. Treating the set of trials in condition A as $\mu$ and condition B as $\nu$, the Wasserstein distance between them is a geometry-aware effect-size measure. Unlike a Euclidean distance between centroids, it is sensitive to changes in trial-to-trial variability structure.

Neural data interpolation. Displacement interpolation along the $W_2$ geodesic between two recordings produces intermediate "synthetic" populations — useful for hypothesizing how representations evolve smoothly across conditions, drug states, or learning stages.

Brain–image alignment (Xiao et al., ICCV 2025). Recent work uses $W_2$ between fMRI voxel embeddings and CLIP-style image embeddings as a cost matrix to construct controlled transport plans for visual decoding. The OT framing helps disentangle redundant from synergistic information in the cross-modal mapping.

6.4 Single-cell and other applications

OT lets you predict how perturbations move cell-state distributions (Klein et al., Schiebinger et al.), align spatial transcriptomics datasets, do shape interpolation in graphics, color transfer in images, and more. The pattern is the same: when your data is naturally a distribution and the geometry of the underlying space is meaningful, OT is a candidate metric.

Module 7 · Hands-on with POT

The POT library (pip install POT) is the de-facto Python toolkit for OT. We'll work through five recipes covering the core operations.

Setup

import numpy as np
import ot              # POT
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

rng = np.random.default_rng(0)

Recipe 1 — Exact OT between two empirical distributions

n, m = 50, 60
xs = rng.normal(loc=0.0, scale=1.0, size=(n, 2))
xt = rng.normal(loc=4.0, scale=1.0, size=(m, 2))

a = np.ones(n) / n
b = np.ones(m) / m
M = ot.dist(xs, xt, metric='sqeuclidean')   # cost matrix

P = ot.emd(a, b, M)                          # exact LP (network simplex)
W2_sq = np.sum(P * M)
print(f"W_2^2 = {W2_sq:.4f}")

Recipe 2 — Sinkhorn (entropic OT)

eps = 0.05 * M.max()
P_eps = ot.sinkhorn(a, b, M, reg=eps, numItermax=500)
print("Sinkhorn cost:", np.sum(P_eps * M))

# Stable log-domain version, useful for small eps
P_log = ot.bregman.sinkhorn_log(a, b, M, reg=eps)

Recipe 3 — Sinkhorn divergence as a loss

def sinkhorn_div(xs, xt, eps):
    n, m = len(xs), len(xt)
    a, b = np.ones(n)/n, np.ones(m)/m
    Mst = ot.dist(xs, xt, metric='sqeuclidean')
    Mss = ot.dist(xs, xs, metric='sqeuclidean')
    Mtt = ot.dist(xt, xt, metric='sqeuclidean')
    s_st = ot.sinkhorn2(a, b, Mst, eps)
    s_ss = ot.sinkhorn2(a, a, Mss, eps)
    s_tt = ot.sinkhorn2(b, b, Mtt, eps)
    return s_st - 0.5 * s_ss - 0.5 * s_tt

print("S_eps =", sinkhorn_div(xs, xt, eps=1.0))

Recipe 4 — Domain adaptation by OT

from sklearn.datasets import make_moons
from sklearn.linear_model import LogisticRegression

# Source: standard moons. Target: rotated and shifted.
Xs, ys = make_moons(n_samples=300, noise=0.05, random_state=0)
theta = np.deg2rad(35)
R = np.array([[np.cos(theta), -np.sin(theta)],
              [np.sin(theta),  np.cos(theta)]])
Xt = Xs @ R.T + np.array([2.5, 1.0])

# Vanilla classifier (trained on source) on target
clf = LogisticRegression().fit(Xs, ys)
print("Naive target accuracy:", clf.score(Xt, ys))

# OT-mapped source -> target, then train
ot_da = ot.da.SinkhornTransport(reg_e=1e-1)
ot_da.fit(Xs=Xs, Xt=Xt)
Xs_t = ot_da.transform(Xs=Xs)
clf2 = LogisticRegression().fit(Xs_t, ys)
print("OT-DA target accuracy:", clf2.score(Xt, ys))

Recipe 5 — Gromov–Wasserstein for unsupervised alignment

# Two point clouds in different spaces (no shared coordinates)
X = rng.normal(size=(40, 5))   # 40 points in 5D
Y = rng.normal(size=(40, 8))   # 40 points in 8D, unrelated coords

C1 = ot.dist(X, X)             # intra-set distance matrices
C2 = ot.dist(Y, Y)
p, q = np.ones(40)/40, np.ones(40)/40

T_gw, log = ot.gromov.gromov_wasserstein(
    C1, C2, p, q, loss_fun='square_loss', log=True)
print("GW distortion:", log['gw_dist'])

# T_gw is the alignment plan. argmax along axis=1 gives a soft assignment.
matches = T_gw.argmax(axis=1)

If you want to teach yourself OT for neural data, do this

  1. Run all five POT recipes above on your own data.
  2. Reproduce a single figure from the GW-OT neuroscience toolbox paper.
  3. Read Peyré & Cuturi chapters 2–4 (theory) and 5 (entropic).
  4. Implement Sinkhorn from scratch in NumPy in log-domain. Compare with POT's output. The exercise of debugging numerical underflow teaches you more than any reading.
  5. Try GW-OT on two sessions of recording. Iterate.
